What I do:

I build applications to make life easier, to make your existing processes more efficient. I try to Whatever tasks make up your business, there's always room to free more of your time back up.
 
By analyzing your business' process, I can determine where and how you can implement automation. You may be able to free up hours, days, or months of work. Whatever the case may be, let me see what I can take off of your plate. There's nothing to lose - I'll hear out what you have for free, and use my experience to make the most impact with the least headache.

Eliminate manual work.

Automating a part or all of your process saves you or your employees time that could be spent on more important tasks. Processes that take hours can be brought to minutes or seconds. Processes that take minutes add up to hours. Anywhere in your business that can be performed automatically adds up, and time is money.

Increase Accuracy.

Humans make mistakes. Robots take the rules that are laid out for them, and perform as built consistently. An automated process is one that isn't prone to human misunderstandings and errors. Re-working a mistake can take more time than the task took initially, automating that process cuts down on that need.

What can I automate?

I deal primarily in Windows applications. I can develop solutions for processes that use most apps (browsers, Microsoft products, in-house built, or anything else) including some cases where the applications are hosted on a virtual machine and accessed remotely. 

Good candidates for automation are repetitive tasks and tasks with clearly-defined rules. For instance: claim processing, report generation, ticket requests & receipts, PDF form building, data entry, and countless other cases. 

Some processes can be automated completely end-to-end, set to run on a schedule and entirely eliminating the requirement for a human being. Others have points where an objective decision is required; it's entirely possible to automate relevant sections broken up to work alongside the user.
